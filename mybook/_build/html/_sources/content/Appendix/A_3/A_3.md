# A.3 LINEAR EQUATIONS


A set of $m$ linear equations in $n$ variables is expressed as

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathrm{a}_{11} \mathrm{x}_{1}+\mathrm{a}_{12} \mathrm{x}_{2}+\ldots+\mathrm{a}_{1 \mathrm{n}} \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{n}}=\mathrm{b}_{1} \\
& \mathrm{a}_{21} \mathrm{x}_{1}+\mathrm{a}_{22} \mathrm{x}_{2}+\ldots+\mathrm{a}_{2 \mathrm{n}} \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{n}}=\mathrm{b}_{2} \\
& \vdots \\
& \vdots \\
& \mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{m} 1} \mathrm{x}_{1}+\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{m} 2} \mathrm{x}_{2}+\ldots+\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{mn}} \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{n}}=\mathrm{b}_{\mathrm{m}}
\end{aligned}
$$

The coefficients of the variables can be written as a matrix A , where

$$
\mathrm{A}=\left[\begin{array}{cccc}
\mathrm{a}_{11} & \mathrm{a}_{12} & \ldots & \mathrm{a}_{1 \mathrm{n}} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\
\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{m} 1} & \mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{m} 2} & \ldots & \mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{mn}}
\end{array}\right]
$$

The variables and right-hand sides of the equations can be written as column vectors, thus, $\mathrm{X}=\left(\mathrm{x}_{1}, \mathrm{x}_{2}, \ldots, \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{n}}\right)$ and $\mathrm{b}=\left(\mathrm{b}_{1}, \mathrm{~b}_{2}, \ldots, \mathrm{~b}_{\mathrm{m}}\right)$. In this context, the matrix A can be viewed as an operation or transformation on the vector X , yielding the resulting vector b . This can be written as $\mathrm{AX}=\mathrm{b}$, and has the same meaning as the set of linear equations depicted earlier.

A solution to this set of linear equations is any vector $X$ that satisfies the equations $\mathrm{AX}=\mathrm{b}$. A unique solution to a set of m independent linear equations in n variables exists if $\mathrm{m}=\mathrm{n}$ and if the inverse of A exists. If $\mathrm{m}>\mathrm{n}$, there may be no solution. And if $\mathrm{m}<\mathrm{n}$, there are infinitely many solutions.

Techniques for solving a system of linear equations may involve the use of so-called elementary row operations on the equations. The application of any of the following row operations yields an equivalent system of equations and may simplify the solution process:

- Any two rows (equations) may be interchanged.
- Any row (equation) may be multiplied by a non-zero constant.
- Any row (equation) may be added to any other row (equation).

When $\mathrm{m}=\mathrm{n}$, if it is possible to transform the matrix of coefficients A into a triangular matrix by performing elementary row operations, then the system can be solved easily. For example if the system of equations appears as

$$
\left[\begin{array}{ccccc}
a_{11} & a_{12} & \ldots & & a_{1 n} \\
0 & a_{22} & & & a_{2 n} \\
0 & 0 & a_{33} & \ldots & a_{3 n} \\
\vdots & & & \ddots & \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \ldots & a_{m n}
\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c}
x_{1} \\
x_{2} \\
x_{3} \\
\vdots \\
x_{n}
\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}
b_{1} \\
b_{2} \\
b_{3} \\
\vdots \\
b_{m}
\end{array}\right]
$$

then we know that $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{mn}} \cdot \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{n}}=\mathrm{b}_{\mathrm{m}}$, so we can easily find a value for $\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{n}}$. Using this, we can then solve for $\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{n}-1}$ in the next to the last equation, and so on until finally we have a value for $\mathrm{x}_{1}$.
An alternative approach to the solution process is to create an augmented matrix $B$ consisting of the coefficient matrix $A$ with one additional column containing the elements $b_{i}$. Then this new matrix $B$ is an $m$ row by ( $n+1$ ) column matrix, and each row of $B$ represents one equation of the system of equations. Next apply the necessary elementary row operations to B that transform the original A portion of B into the identity matrix I . This will have the effect of causing the $b$ portion of $B$ to be transformed into a vector representing the solution to the system of equations.
