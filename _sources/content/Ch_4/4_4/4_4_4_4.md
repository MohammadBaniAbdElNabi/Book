# 4.4.4.4 The Impact of Model Formulation

For linear programming models, it does not make much difference how the original problem is formulated, provided that the objective function and the constraints are correct. In integer programming, however, the formulation itself can have a dramatic effect on algorithm performance. As an example, consider the original problem formulation:

$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\operatorname{maximize} & \mathrm{z}=600 \mathrm{x}_{1}+100 \mathrm{x}_{2} \\
\text { subject to: } & 150 \mathrm{x}_{1}+10 \mathrm{x}_{2}+\mathrm{x}_{3}=300 \\
& 300 \mathrm{x}_{1}+400 \mathrm{x}_{2}+\mathrm{x}_{4}=1800 \\
& \mathrm{x}_{1}, \mathrm{x}_{2} \geq 0 \text { and integer }
\end{array}
$$

Observe that, for any feasible integer solution to this problem, $x_{3}$ must be a multiple of 10 , and $\mathrm{x}_{4}$ must be a multiple of 100 . Suppose we first reduced the original problem to lowest common terms (before adding the slack variables):

$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\operatorname{maximize} & \mathrm{z}=6 \mathrm{x}_{1}+\mathrm{x}_{2} \\
\text { subject to: } & 15 \mathrm{x}_{1}+\mathrm{x}_{2}+\mathrm{x}_{3}=30 \\
& 3 \mathrm{x}_{1}+4 \mathrm{x}_{2}+\mathrm{x}_{4}=18 \\
& \mathrm{x}_{1}, \mathrm{x}_{2} \geq 0 \text { and integer }
\end{array}
$$

This new problem is identical to the original as far as the LP is concerned, but it is not the same integer problem! The new optimal Simplex tableau is:

| Basis | $\mathrm{x}_{1}$ | $\mathrm{x}_{2}$ | $\mathrm{x}_{3}$ | $\mathrm{x}_{4}$ | Solution |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Z | 0 | 0 | $7 / 19$ | $3 / 19$ | $13 \frac{17}{19}$ |
| $\mathrm{x}_{1}$ | 1 | 0 | $4 / 57$ | $-1 / 57$ | $1 \frac{15}{19}$ |
| $\mathrm{x}_{2}$ | 0 | 1 | $-1 / 19$ | $5 / 19$ | $3 \frac{3}{19}$ |

The most obvious immediate consequence of this new formulation is simply that z must be an integer multiple of 100. The upper bound on z denoted $\overline{\mathrm{z}}$ is now 1,300. The reduction has no effect on the up and down penalties except that we get the decrease in the reduced units of z . Because the optimal value of z must be integer, the up and down penalties can be strengthened. For example, $\mathrm{D}_{1}$ in the old version reduced z to 975 . Using the new tableau, z will become 9.75 , which can be replaced by 9 as an upper bound on the down problem. Since the initial rounded solution is $\mathrm{z}=9$, the corresponding branch is fathomed, that is, we can branch up on $\mathrm{x}_{1}$ for free. The complete revised branch-and-bound tree with up and down penalties is illustrated in Figure 4.7.

Notice also that the slack variables, $\mathrm{x}_{3}$ and $\mathrm{x}_{4}$ are integer valued in both problems and they will be candidates for branching. The slack and surplus variables (and the objective function variable z) will always be integer valued (in a pure integer problem) if all of the problem coefficients are integer. Thus, for example, if one of the constraint coefficients is 0.5 , it would be advantageous to multiply the corresponding constraint by 2 to produce all integer coefficients. In general, any rational fractions can be removed by multiplying by the denominator. Refer to Johnson et al. (2000) for additional formulations.
