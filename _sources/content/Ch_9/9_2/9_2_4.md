# 9.2.4 Hurwicz Principle


Define $0 \leq \alpha \leq 1$ to be the Decision-maker's Degree of Optimism between the two extremes of Maximin $(\alpha=0)$ and Maximax $(\alpha=1)$. For each alternative, $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{j}}$, define the Hurwicz measure:

$$
\mathrm{h}_{\mathrm{j}}=\alpha\left\{\max _{\mathrm{i}} \mathrm{p}_{\mathrm{ij}}\right\}+(1-\alpha)\left\{\min _{\mathrm{i}} \mathrm{p}_{\mathrm{ij}}\right\}
$$

where $\mathrm{p}_{\mathrm{ij}}$ represents the payoff associated with alternative $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{j}}$ and state $\Theta_{\mathrm{i}}$. Select the alternative with the highest value of $\mathrm{h}_{\mathrm{j}}$.

The Hurwicz principle is based on the assumption that the decision-maker is neither totally pessimistic (as with the Maximin strategy), nor totally optimistic (as with Maximax). Each individual decision-maker can select his own degree of optimism somewhere between these two extremes. Observe that when $\alpha=1$, then $\mathrm{h}_{\mathrm{j}}$ will simply be the maximum payoff for alternative, $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{j}}$, and Hurwicz will be equivalent to the optimistic Maximax rule. Similarly, when $\alpha=0, \mathrm{~h}_{\mathrm{j}}$ will be the minimum payoff for alternative $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{j}}$. In this case, by selecting the largest $\mathrm{h}_{\mathrm{j}}$, the decision-maker is choosing the pessimistic, conservative Maximin alternative. However, when some intermediate value of a is chosen, we get an alternative that balances the risk against the potential gains. In the example, suppose we choose $\alpha=0.6$. For alternative $a_{5}$, the maximum payoff is 7 , the minimum payoff is 2 , and the corresponding Hurwicz value is $\mathrm{h}_{5}=0.6 *\{7\}+0.4 *\{2\}=5$. The values of the Hurwicz measure for the first five alternatives are $3,3,4.8,4$, and 5 , respectively. By picking the maximum of these, we determine that the best strategy is to choose alternative $a_{5}$.
