# A.4 QUADRATIC FORMS


Let $A_{n \times n}$ be a symmetric matrix, and $X$ be an $n$-element vector. The function $f(X)$ defined as

$$
f(X)=X^{T} A X
$$

is called a quadratic form. Since $\mathrm{X}^{\mathrm{T}}$ is of order $1 \times \mathrm{n}$ and A is $\mathrm{n} \times \mathrm{n}$ and X is $\mathrm{n} \times 1$, the product

$$
\left[x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cccc}
a_{11} & a_{12} & \ldots & a_{1 n} \\
& & \vdots & \\
a_{n 1} & a_{n 2} & \ldots & a_{n n}
\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c}
x_{1} \\
\vdots \\
x_{n}
\end{array}\right]
$$

exists and can be computed. Clearly, $\mathrm{f}(\mathrm{X})$ is a scalar value, and can be written as

$$
f(X)=\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{i j} x_{i} x_{j}
$$

Thus, $\mathrm{f}(\mathrm{X})$ is a sum of quadratic terms, and hence the name quadratic form.
In this context, the matrix A has one of the following characteristics:
Positive definite: If $\mathrm{f}(\mathrm{X})>0$ for all $\mathrm{X} \neq 0$
Positive semidefinite: If $\mathrm{f}(\mathrm{X}) \geq 0$ for all X and there exists an $\mathrm{X} \neq 0$ for which $\mathrm{f}(\mathrm{X})=0$

Negative definite: If $\mathrm{f}(\mathrm{X})<0$ for all $\mathrm{X} \neq 0$
Negative semidefinite: If $\mathrm{f}(\mathrm{X}) \leq 0$ for all X and there exists an $\mathrm{X} \neq 0$ for which $\mathrm{f}(\mathrm{X})=0$ Indefinite: If none of the above
