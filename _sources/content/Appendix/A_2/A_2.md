# A.2 MATRICES AND MATRIX OPERATIONS


A real matrix is a rectangular array of real numbers. Subscripts, such as i and j , can be used to index the rows and columns, respectively. A matrix A of m rows and n columns is called an $\mathrm{m} \times \mathrm{n}$ (" m by $\mathrm{n}^{\prime \prime}$ ) matrix and is written as:

$$
A=\left(a_{i j}\right)=\left[\begin{array}{cccc}
a_{11} & a_{12} & \ldots & a_{1 n} \\
a_{21} & a_{22} & \ldots & a_{2 n} \\
\vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\
a_{m 1} & a_{m 2} & \ldots & a_{m n}
\end{array}\right]
$$

where $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ij}}$ denotes the element in the i -th row and the j -th column. Any matrix A can be multiplied by a scalar $\alpha$ with the result that every element $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ij}}$ in A becomes the value $\alpha \mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ij}}$ in the scalar product matrix.

Two matrices $\mathrm{A}_{\mathrm{m} \times \mathrm{n}}$ and $\mathrm{B}_{\mathrm{p} \times \mathrm{q}}$ can be added if $\mathrm{m}=\mathrm{p}$ and $\mathrm{n}=\mathrm{q}$. The sum $\mathrm{C}=\mathrm{A}+\mathrm{B}$ is a matrix $C_{m \times n}$ in which the element $c_{i j}$ is computed as $\left(a_{i j}+b_{i j}\right)$. Two matrices $A_{m \times n}$ and $B_{p \times q}$ may be multiplied if $\mathrm{n}=\mathrm{p}$. The product $\mathrm{C}=\mathrm{AB}$ is defined to be a matrix $\mathrm{C}_{\mathrm{m} \times \mathrm{q}}$ in which the element $\mathrm{c}_{\mathrm{ij}}$ is computed as:

$$
\mathrm{c}_{\mathrm{ij}}=\sum_{\mathrm{k}=1}^{\mathrm{n}} \mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ik}} \mathrm{~b}_{\mathrm{kj}}
$$

For the special case in which $\mathrm{m}=\mathrm{q}=1$, we actually have just the product of two vectors. This product is called the inner product or dot product, and the dot product of two vectors $\mathrm{X}=\left(\mathrm{x}_{1}, \mathrm{x}_{2}, \ldots, \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{n}}\right)$ and $\mathrm{Y}=\left(\mathrm{y}_{1}, \mathrm{y}_{2}, \ldots, \mathrm{y}_{\mathrm{n}}\right)$ is denoted and defined by:

$$
\mathrm{X} \bullet \mathrm{Y}=\left(\mathrm{x}_{1} \mathrm{y}_{1}+\mathrm{x}_{2} \mathrm{y}_{2}+\ldots+\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{n}} \mathrm{y}_{\mathrm{n}}\right)
$$

which is consistent with the definition of general matrix multiplication.

Matrix addition and multiplication exhibit some of the properties of real arithmetic operations. For example, for matrices $\mathrm{A}, \mathrm{B}$, and C , the following properties hold:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathrm{A}+\mathrm{B}=\mathrm{B}+\mathrm{A} \\
& (\mathrm{~A}+\mathrm{B})+\mathrm{C}=\mathrm{A}+(\mathrm{B}+\mathrm{C}) \\
& \mathrm{A}(\mathrm{~B}+\mathrm{C})=\mathrm{AB}+\mathrm{AC} \\
& (\mathrm{AB}) \mathrm{C}=\mathrm{A}(\mathrm{BC})
\end{aligned}
$$

However, note that, in general, matrix multiplication is not commutative, so $\mathrm{AB} \neq \mathrm{BA}$ in general, and in fact these products may not even exist.
The transpose of an $\mathrm{m} \times \mathrm{n}$ matrix A is the $\mathrm{n} \times \mathrm{m}$ matrix $\mathrm{A}^{\mathrm{T}}$ obtained by interchanging the roles of the rows and columns in A . Thus, if A is the matrix shown earlier, then $\mathrm{A}^{\mathrm{T}}$ is the matrix whose elements have the same values as those in A, but arranged in the form

$$
\left[\begin{array}{cccc}
\mathrm{a}_{11} & \mathrm{a}_{21} & \cdots & \mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{m} 1} \\
\mathrm{a}_{12} & \mathrm{a}_{22} & \cdots & \mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{m} 2} \\
\vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\
\mathrm{a}_{1 \mathrm{n}} & \mathrm{a}_{2 \mathrm{n}} & \cdots & \mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{mn}}
\end{array}\right]
$$

Reversing the roles twice simply yields the original matrix, so for any matrix A , $\left(\mathrm{A}^{\mathrm{T}}\right)^{\mathrm{T}}=\mathrm{A}$.
For example, the following two matrices are the transpose of each other:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{lll}
3 & 2 & 4 \\
7 & 1 & 5
\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ll}
3 & 7 \\
2 & 1 \\
4 & 5
\end{array}\right]
$$

A property of matrix multiplication and transposition is that $(\mathrm{AB})^{\mathrm{T}}=\mathrm{B}^{\mathrm{T}} \mathrm{A}^{\mathrm{T}}$.
A square matrix is one for which $\mathrm{m}=\mathrm{n}$. The main diagonal of a square matrix A is the set of elements $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ij}}$ for which $\mathrm{i}=\mathrm{j}$, that is, $\mathrm{a}_{11}, \mathrm{a}_{22}, \ldots, \mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{nn}}$. An $\mathrm{n} \times \mathrm{n}$ matrix A is symmetric about the main diagonal if every element $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ij}}$ is equal to the element $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ji}}$. A square matrix A is triangular (or upper triangular) if all the elements below the main diagonal have value zero; that is, $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ij}}=0$ for all $\mathrm{i}>\mathrm{j}$. For example, the following matrix is upper triangular:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{llll}
4 & 6 & 2 & 5 \\
0 & 7 & 1 & 2 \\
0 & 0 & 4 & 3 \\
0 & 0 & 0 & 8
\end{array}\right]
$$

A matrix is lower triangular if all the elements above the main diagonal have value zero.
The identity matrix I is an $\mathrm{n} \times \mathrm{n}$ matrix whose columns are the standard basis, and in which the i-th column contains the i-th unit vector. The identity matrix contains ones along the main diagonal ( $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ii}}=1$ for all i ) and zeros elsewhere ( $\mathrm{a}_{\mathrm{ij}}=0$ for all $\mathrm{i} \neq \mathrm{j}$ ). This matrix has the property that $\mathrm{AI}=\mathrm{IA}=\mathrm{A}$ for any $\mathrm{n} \times \mathrm{n}$ real matrix A .

The rank of a matrix A is the number of linearly independent rows (or columns) in A , and is denoted as $\mathbf{r a n k}$ (A). A square matrix $\mathrm{A}_{\mathrm{n} \times \mathrm{n}}$ having rank n (full rank) is called a nonsingular matrix.

A square matrix A may have an inverse matrix $\mathrm{A}^{-1}$ such that $\mathrm{AA}^{-1}=\mathrm{A}^{-1} \mathrm{~A}=\mathrm{I}$. Such an inverse exists if and only if $\operatorname{rank}(\mathrm{A})=\mathrm{n}$ (or equivalently, if and only if A is non-singular), and in that case, the inverse $\mathrm{A}^{-1}$ is unique. The inverse of the inverse of a matrix A is the original matrix A; thus, $\left(\mathrm{A}^{-1}\right)^{-1}=\mathrm{A}$. And if two matrices A and B have inverses $\mathrm{A}^{-1}$ and $\mathrm{B}^{-1}$, respectively, then

$$
(A B)^{-1}=B^{-1} A^{-1}
$$
